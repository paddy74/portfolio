# Peersonal Grammar

This article documents reference material and personal preferences for crafting well-written documents.

I try to align with the general prescriptive American grammar whenever I am writing, whether it be technical, creative, or personal in nature. However, I am not a linguist, grammarian, or even someone paticularly exceptional at writing. So where I find my descriptive grammar and understanding of the appropriate vernacular to be lacking to convey meaning in the style I want, more authoritative sources are needed.

This article is my attempt to capture _my personal_ "correct" way to write &mdash; combined from my experience, education, and published authorities (e.g. the [American Psychological Association](https://www.apa.org/)) &mdash; so that I can continuously improve and share my motivations with colleagues.

## Em Dashes, En Dashes, and Hyphens

[:link: Be dashing &mdash; and do it well](https://www.merriam-webster.com/grammar/em-dash-en-dash-how-to-use)[^1]

[^1]: [_Be dashing &mdash; and do it well_, Merriam-Webster](https://www.merriam-webster.com/grammar/em-dash-en-dash-how-to-use)

- **Em Dash (&mdash;)**: The longest dash, used to create a strong break in a sentence &mdash; often as an alternative to commas, colons, or parentheses. An em dash emphasizes information or add an adrupt change in thought.
- **En Dash (&ndash;)**: Only slightly longer than a hyphen, an en dash is used to indicate ranges (e.g. numbers or dates) or connections between related items (e.g. "the New York&ndash;London flight").
- **Hyphen (-)**: The shortest dash, a hyphen is used to connect words or parts of words, such as in combound adjectives (e.g. "a well-known author") or to break one word at the end of a line.
